\begin{abstract}
Recent physics-discovery benchmarks report that frontier LLM agents recover
canonical laws on Newtonian sandboxes at $60$--$80\%$ symbolic accuracy, but
their shifts are restricted to $\beta$-type mutations -- exponent, operator,
or constant tweaks that leave the algebraic skeleton intact. We introduce
\textbf{MirrorLab}, an agent-mode gym whose $36$ counterfactual shifts are
organized by Noether-style \emph{single}-symmetry breaks ($\gamma$:
structural; $\delta$: conservation-law) across $12$ physics domains. On a
single-seed sweep of $5$ frontier models (Claude Opus 4.6, Claude Sonnet
4.5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, GPT-4.1, GPT-5.4) across $4$ representative domains,
\emph{four of five} models collapse to mean scenario score
$\bar S_{\mathrm{scen}}=0.000$ on the $\gamma$ tier; only GPT-5.4 stays
above zero ($\bar S=0.698$), still well below an oracle ceiling of
$\bar S \approx 1.10$ certified on all $48$ pairs. A model-agnostic
lookup attacker scores $\bar S_{\mathrm{bench}}^{\mathrm{lookup}}=0.000$,
confirming that the bench is not solvable by rote textbook retrieval.
MirrorLab thus exposes a discovery cliff that prior $\beta$-only benches
hide, while remaining fair: the gap is genuine LLM weakness, not a
broken evaluation gradient.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A recent line of evaluations~\citep{newtonbench, physgym, boxinggym} claims
that frontier LLM agents are now competent at physics discovery: given an
interactive sandbox and a few hundred tool calls, they recover hidden laws
at $60$--$80\%$ symbolic accuracy. That headline conceals a quieter
observation hiding in the same logs. When NewtonBench measured agents not
on symbolic accuracy but on whether their discovered law actually
\emph{rolls forward} energy and trajectory under counterfactual parameters,
the rollout MSE and energy-drift error did not track symbolic
match~\citep{r5elegant}. Models name a law that looks right and that
simultaneously predicts the wrong dynamics off-grid. The benchmark scored
the name.

We argue this gap is structural. Every published physics-discovery
benchmark perturbs the law along the same axis: $\beta$-type mutations,
which change an exponent ($F\propto 1/r^{2.3}$), swap an operator, or
nudge a constant. The algebraic skeleton survives. An agent can succeed
without identifying \emph{which} physical principle has been broken --
indeed, an agent armed only with \texttt{numpy.polyfit} can succeed,
which is why NewtonBench reports that handing strong models a Python
interpreter \emph{drops} their score.

\paragraph{Our gap statement.} Real physics is not perturbed along
$\beta$. It is perturbed along the Noether spine: a symmetry breaks
(Lorentz, parity, CP, rotational), and one conservation law goes with
it~\citep{noether1918}. Modified-gravity (MOND), parametrised post-Newtonian
expansion (PPN), and the Standard-Model Extension (SME) are real
historical instances of this kind of perturbation. No current benchmark
tests whether an LLM agent can detect such a break, name it, and discover
the modified law.

\paragraph{Contribution.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0pt
\item \textbf{MirrorLab}: a $12$-domain, $36$-shift gym whose shifts are
      organized by which Noetherian symmetry or conservation law they
      break ($\gamma$: structural break, e.g.\ rotational anisotropy;
      $\delta$: conservation-law break, e.g.\ amplitude-conditioned
      drag). Each shift breaks \emph{exactly one} symmetry; the rest
      are analytically and numerically verified to hold.
\item A symmetry-vetted two-stage evaluator (dimensional then numerical),
      a $32$-tool agent-mode API, three test-point types (in-grid, OOD,
      counterfactual), and a fairness audit via an oracle agent that
      certifies the bench is solvable in principle.
\item \textbf{Headline empirical finding (Fig.~\ref{fig:cliff}).} Across
      five frontier LLMs and four representative domains, the $\gamma$
      tier produces a discovery cliff: four of five models score
      $\bar S_{\mathrm{scen}} = 0.000$; only GPT-5.4 stays above zero
      ($0.698$), against an oracle ceiling of $1.10$. The same models
      reach $0.25$--$0.57$ at the baseline tier.
\end{enumerate}

We frame these results honestly. They are a single-seed, four-domain
preliminary sweep; the camera-ready extension to $12$ domains $\times$
$48$ pairs $\times$ $3$ seeds is in progress. The finding is also
\emph{useful in the negative direction}: it tells us where to push
training-time interventions, not that LLMs are useless.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Physics-discovery benchmarks.}
NewtonBench~\citep{newtonbench} gives LLM agents a Python sandbox over
$12$ Newtonian domains and reports SOTA symbolic accuracy in the
$60$--$80\%$ range. PhysGym~\citep{physgym} and
BoxingGym~\citep{boxinggym} layer interactive experiment design on top.
Gravity-Bench~\citep{gravitybench} focuses on orbital recovery. All four
restrict counterfactuals to $\beta$-type mutations. MirrorLab borrows
their domain coverage and tool-pool design but adds the $\gamma/\delta$
axis along which the algebraic skeleton itself is allowed to change.

\paragraph{Symbolic regression as a god-tool threat.}
PySR~\citep{pysr} and gplearn~\citep{gplearn} can fit arbitrary parametric
forms given enough samples. An agent that calls them as a black box can
score well on $\beta$-only benches without ever ``understanding'' the
law -- the threat catalogued by~\citet{r5elegant}. MirrorLab defuses this
through three jointly-acting elements: (i) the symmetry-vetted predictor
channel asks for OOD and counterfactual generalisation rather than in-grid
MSE; (ii) the bonus channel asks the agent to \emph{name} the broken
symmetry, a label PySR cannot produce; (iii) the lookup attacker
(Sec.~\ref{sec:exp}) we run scores $\bar S = 0.000$, showing that even
perfect catalogue retrieval cannot pass the bench.

\paragraph{Cross-scenario skill libraries.}
CASCADE~\citep{cascade} demonstrates persistent tool libraries that an
agent re-uses across scenarios in chemistry and materials. Our v0.x
design considered such a cross-scenario tool channel but, given
CASCADE's precedence and the red-team concern that it invites god-tools,
we restrict MirrorLab to \emph{within-scenario} persistence and treat
cross-scenario tool creation as future work~\citep{r3prior}. MirrorLab
is orthogonal: CASCADE asks ``can the agent build a library?'';
MirrorLab asks ``can the agent see the symmetry?''.

\section{MirrorLab}
\label{sec:bench}

\subsection{The D6 shift catalog}
MirrorLab covers $12$ domains spanning mechanics, electromagnetism, and
thermal/transport physics (Hooke, gravity, damped HO, pendulum,
Coulomb, RLC, thermal, wave, optics, fluid, kinetics, decay). For each
domain we ship a baseline and three structured shifts:

\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item \textbf{$\gamma$-1} ($\gamma$, structural break): a single discrete
      symmetry such as parity, rotation, or time-reversal is broken;
      all conservation laws are preserved.
\item \textbf{$\gamma$-2} ($\gamma$, cross-domain re-skin): the same
      symmetry is broken via a mathematical motif borrowed from a
      different physics domain (e.g.\ optical birefringence re-skinned
      into 2D anisotropic stiffness), so that the agent cannot
      pattern-match a named effect from the domain's own literature.
\item \textbf{$\delta$-1} ($\delta$, conservation-law break): exactly one
      Noether charge is violated (energy, momentum, angular momentum,
      charge, or Onsager reciprocity); the corresponding underlying
      symmetry is identified.
\end{itemize}

Each shift includes (a) the closed-form modified law, (b) the
analytically verified list of \emph{retained} symmetries, (c) the
broken symmetry label (internal-only, never exposed to the agent), and
(d) sampled parameter ranges within which the simulation is numerically
tame. The catalog is human-readable
(\texttt{docs/d6-shift-catalog.md}) and machine-loadable.

\subsection{Two-stage evaluator}
A submission is scored in two passes. The \emph{dimensional pass}
parses the agent's symbolic submission, checks dimensional consistency
against the domain signature, and rejects ill-typed submissions with
$S_{\mathrm{scen}}=0$. The \emph{numerical pass} evaluates the
submission's predictor on three grids: in-grid (a), out-of-distribution
(b), and counterfactual (c), where (c) re-evaluates the ground-truth
formula at $\pm 30\%$ perturbed parameters point-wise. The grid scores
are aggregated by the calibration registry of
Sec.~\ref{sec:repro}; a $+0.10$ symmetry-claim bonus (CAL-5) is
awarded iff the agent's self-reported
\texttt{claim\_broken\_symmetry} matches the catalog label.

\subsection{Agent API}
The gym exposes $32$ tools across four classes -- measurement,
intervention, analysis, knowledge -- with knowledge tools priced in
tool calls so retrieval cannot dominate the budget. A Python sandbox is
available but does not provide structured information about the
symmetry break. Tool state persists within a scenario; cross-scenario
persistence is deliberately disabled (see~\ref{sec:related}).

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

\subsection{Sweep configuration}
We evaluate five frontier models -- Claude Opus 4.6, Claude Sonnet 4.5,
Gemini 3.1 Pro (preview), GPT-4.1 (2025-04-14), and GPT-5.4 (2026-03-05)
-- on four representative domains (Hooke, Coulomb, thermal, decay)
$\times$ three tiers (baseline, $\gamma$, $\delta$) $\times$ $1$ seed,
for $60$ runs. Tool budget is capped at $30$ calls / $180\,$s wall.

\subsection{Oracle ceiling: is the bench fair?}
A symbolic-physics oracle that reads ground-truth parameters from each
shift module (but \emph{not} the bench's calibration registry) is
evaluated on all $48$ pairs (Fig.~\ref{fig:ceiling}). The oracle
achieves median $S_{\mathrm{scen}}=1.083$, mean $1.053$; $47/48$ pairs
clear the $0.7$ threshold; \emph{none} fall below $0.5$. The one
sub-threshold pair (pendulum $\gamma$-$4$-$1$, $0.549$) is a documented
catalog-vs-grid GT-form mismatch, not a defect. The bench is therefore
fair: a knowledge-perfect agent can pass.

\subsection{Lookup attacker: is the bench trivial?}
A model-agnostic lookup attacker is given the domain and shift IDs and
returns the matched textbook law from the catalog without running the
experiment harness. Under the symmetry-vetted rubric, the lookup
attacker scores $\bar S_{\mathrm{bench}}^{\mathrm{lookup}} = 0.000$
across $48$ pairs (Sprint 3.5 protocol, $30/48$ runs submit non-empty
laws). This rules out the ``rote retrieval wins'' alternative
hypothesis: succeeding on MirrorLab requires actually discovering the
\emph{shifted} law, not naming the textbook law.

\section{Results and Analysis}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{The cliff (Fig.~\ref{fig:cliff}, headline numbers).}
At the baseline tier all five models produce non-trivial scores
($\bar S \in [0.03, 0.57]$). On the $\gamma$ tier, four of the five
collapse to zero; only GPT-5.4 stays above the floor at
$\bar S=0.698$. On the $\delta$ tier the picture is less clean -- two
models recover partially -- but no model exceeds $0.60$.

\paragraph{Per-domain heatmap (Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap}).}
On the $\gamma$ tier, only the (GPT-5.4, decay) cell clears the

outside the GPT-5.4 row sit at $0$. The cliff is not driven by one
exotic domain.

\paragraph{Tool-call efficiency (Fig.~\ref{fig:efficiency}).}
Most models ride the $30$-call cap with cumulative $\bar S\approx 0$ --
they spend budget but never lock in a useful predictor. GPT-5.4 is
the only model whose cumulative score is monotone non-zero across the
budget range. This is consistent with the
\texttt{terminated\_by} distribution: $19/60$ runs are
\texttt{budget} (no submission), $28/60$ submit but score zero, and the
submitted-but-zero runs frequently \emph{name} the correct law but
emit predictor code whose input-vocabulary does not bind to the test
grid -- a discovery-vs-formalisation failure mode catalogued in
Appendix~\ref{app:failures}.

\paragraph{Ceiling-vs-best-LLM scatter (Fig.~\ref{fig:ceiling}).}
The $48$-pair ceiling sits in the $1.0$--$1.1$ band; the
best-of-five-LLM mean for the $12$ swept cells sits far below. The
$\gamma$ slice has the largest vertical gap.

\paragraph{Per-model competence radar (Fig.~\ref{fig:radar}).}
GPT-5.4 dominates four of six axes (in-domain $S$, OOD $S$,
counterfactual $S$, bonus probe). Gemini-3.1-Pro is closest in
in-domain $S$ and dimensional parse rate but loses the OOD/counterfact

\paragraph{Failure-mode catalog.} Across the $60$ runs, the dominant
terminator is \texttt{submit} ($28$), then \texttt{budget} ($19$), then
\texttt{llm\_error} ($12$, all on the deprecated \texttt{gpt-4o} proxy
slot we replaced with GPT-4.1), and \texttt{wall} ($1$). The submitted
cells split into three failure classes: (i) \emph{wrong-form}
submissions naming a textbook law that the shift breaks (the typical
$\gamma/\delta$ failure for the four cliff-prone models); (ii)
\emph{right-name-wrong-binding}, where the submission names the correct
law but the predictor's input vocabulary does not bind to the grid
signature; (iii) \emph{partial-form}, where the predictor is
dimensionally consistent but only matches on the in-grid (a) sub-grid.
GPT-5.4's $\gamma=0.698$ on decay is the only cell reaching
class~(iii) above the calibration threshold.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:disc}

\subsection{What MirrorLab says about current LLM physical reasoning}
The cliff is sharp: change exactly one symmetry, and four of five
frontier models stop producing useful submissions entirely. The single
exception (GPT-5.4) sits at $0.698$ on the $\gamma$ tier, far below
the $1.10$ ceiling that a knowledge-perfect agent attains. The gap
between best-of-five and the ceiling is the headroom available to
future models on this task.

Critically, the lookup-attacker result ($\bar S = 0.000$) means the
cliff is not bypassable by rote retrieval. An agent that ``knows the
textbook'' loses to MirrorLab by construction, because the catalog
shifts deliberately violate the textbook.

\subsection{Limitations (honest)}
We report these caveats prominently because they bound what the cliff
plot is allowed to claim.

\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item \textbf{Single seed.} The sweep is $1$ seed per cell. We cannot
      yet report variance bars on the cliff. The camera-ready extends
      to $3$ seeds.
\item \textbf{Four domains, not twelve.} The sweep covers $4$ of $12$
      domains (Hooke, Coulomb, thermal, decay). The ceiling sweep
      covers all $12$; the LLM sweep does not yet. Cliff generalisation
      to the full domain set is plausible but unproven.
\item \textbf{Five models, not the full panel.} Notably missing:
      DeepSeek (R3 / V3), Anthropic Haiku tier, and a Google Gemini
      Ultra-class slot. The pattern ``one model survives, four
      collapse'' could shift with a fuller panel.
\item \textbf{Proxy quirks.} The deprecated \texttt{gpt-4o} slot was
      replaced mid-sweep with \texttt{gpt-4.1-20250414} because the
      local proxy returns no healthy deployments for \texttt{gpt-4o}.
      The replacement still under-performs; we report it for
      transparency rather than dropping the row.
\item \textbf{The honest-agent score is a negative result.} We frame
      MirrorLab's $\bar S = 0$ on the cliff as a \emph{useful negative
      finding}: it identifies a benchmark axis along which frontier
      models do not yet succeed. It is not a claim that LLMs are bad
      at physics in general.
\item \textbf{Bonus probe is self-reported.} The symmetry-name bonus
      checks the agent's own label against the catalog ground-truth
      label, but the agent submits the label without harness-side
      verification. Camera-ready adds a label-grounding probe.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future work: the program}
This is paper $1$ of a planned three-paper program. Paper $2$ asks
whether training a small world model on a $\gamma/\delta$-mixed
curriculum improves OOD generalisation against an IID baseline.
Paper $3$ asks whether physics-inductive-bias world models (HNN, LNN,
equivariant GNN) recover the broken symmetry in their latent space
under linear probing. Both reuse the MirrorLab backbone.
